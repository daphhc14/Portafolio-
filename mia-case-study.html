Mia — AI Avatar Assistant for Mercer Marsh McLennan

Designing a conversational AI avatar that replaces traditional support agents, helping Mexican employees navigate medical insurance claims through a familiar video-call interface.

Role UX/UI Designer
Platform Mobile Web App

01 · Context

The brief: replace a support agent
with an AI you'd actually want to talk to

Mercer Marsh McLennan asked us to design an AI assistant to help their policy holders navigate the medical claims process. But this wasn't a chatbot brief — they wanted something more human: an avatar that could replace a live support agent, someone users could speak with as if they were on a real call.

The challenge was significant. The target audience was primarily Mexican adults — many of them unfamiliar with AI interactions, skeptical of automation, and accustomed to resolving sensitive matters with real people. Designing an AI that this audience would trust and feel comfortable with required thinking far beyond the interface.

02 · Research

What stood between users
and their claims

Before designing anything, I conducted research through surveys and interviews with people who regularly interact with medical insurance forms. Three clear barriers emerged consistently across users:

Medical and insurance jargon

Users frequently didn't understand what was being asked of them, leading to errors, rejected claims, and calls to support.

Fear of making mistakes

The stakes felt high. A wrong submission could mean a delayed or denied reimbursement, causing anxiety throughout the process.

Distrust of full automation

Users were skeptical of systems with no human fallback — they needed to know a real person was still reachable.

Low AI familiarity

Many users had never interacted with an AI assistant for anything sensitive. Adoption couldn't be assumed — it had to be earned.

The benchmark also confirmed that existing solutions in this space offered little guidance, used opaque language, and provided no sense of accompaniment. There was a clear opportunity to design something that felt genuinely supportive.

04 · Design Strategy

Designing an avatar users
would actually trust

With the personas defined, the core design challenge became clear: how do you make an AI feel trustworthy, approachable, and familiar to an audience that has never used one for something this important?

The answer came from leaning into patterns users already knew. A video call. Everyone has been on one. The interface, the controls, the idea of looking at someone while you speak — it's familiar even to less tech-savvy users. That became the design foundation. From there, three strategic pillars guided every decision:

1

Make the avatar feel like a real person — specifically, a Mexican professional

We designed the avatar, named her Mia, and gave her a professional but warm appearance deliberately chosen to reflect the target audience. She wears a blazer, has dark hair, speaks Spanish with a Mexican accent, and uses natural, everyday language — not insurance terminology. We also cloned a voice that matched her persona: professional, clear, and friendly. Every aspect of her identity was designed intentionally so users would see someone they could relate to.

2

Use a video-call metaphor as the interaction pattern

Rather than designing a new interaction paradigm, I mapped the experience onto something users already understood: a video call. The layout, the bottom controls, the sense of "being on a call with someone" — all of it leveraged existing mental models so users could focus on their task rather than learning a new interface.

3

Always keep a human exit available

Given the skepticism toward full automation in our user research, we made a deliberate decision to keep a human support option visible at all times. The help button surfaces a support executive's phone number and email, making it clear that Mia is a first point of contact — not a replacement for human judgment.

05 · The Interface

Four controls, one avatar, total clarity

The main call screen was designed to be as uncluttered as possible. The avatar fills the screen. At the bottom, a minimal control bar with four actions: help, chat, microphone toggle, and end call. Nothing more.

screen-privacy

Privacy gate

Antes de comenzar

Privacy notice and consent screen before the session starts. The "Comenzar" button is disabled until the user accepts — keeping legal requirements visible without burying them.

screen-main-call

Main call screen

Mia — active session

The avatar fills the screen with a warm purple-to-blue gradient background. Mic active (pink), chat, help, and end call controls sit in a frosted bottom bar.

screen-loading

Loading state

Cargando

A subtle loading indicator overlaid on the avatar signals that the system is processing — keeping the user informed without hiding Mia or disrupting the experience.

screen-chat

Chat panel

Chatea con Mia

A slide-in chat panel lets users switch to text if they're in a noisy environment. Mia's responses appear as speech bubbles — reinforcing the conversation metaphor.

screen-mic-off

Mic off state

Tu micrófono está silenciado

When the mic is off, the entire UI communicates it: the icon turns red, a clear message appears over the avatar, and a contextual tooltip guides the user to enable permissions.

screen-help-modal

Help modal

Ayuda — human support

A clean modal surfaces the support executive's phone number and email with a copy button. Available at any time, it ensures users never feel stuck with only the AI.

07 · Iterations

What we fixed after testing

User testing surfaced real friction. We prioritized fixes using a MoSCoW framework, separating what had to ship from what could wait.

moscow-board MoSCoW prioritization from testing session
Must have
  • Session expiry pop-up with 30-second countdown and option to continue or exit
  • Improved avatar pause detection — no longer interrupting mid-thought
  • Mic status notifications with clear permission guidance
  • Better name/language distinction logic in the avatar's intro
  • Avatar explains upfront how it can help (context-setting)
  • Speed of voice adjusted — more natural and intelligible
Should have
  • After 3 failed attempts to understand, automatically redirect to human agent
  • Clearer instruction for confirming user name by voice
  • Better detection of negative sentiment ("not all my questions were answered")
Nice to have
  • Background noise reduction
  • Reduced microphone sensitivity (volume threshold)
  • Option to view the form while speaking with the avatar
Won't have (MVP)
  • No items deprioritized to won't-have — all identified issues had a viable path to resolution

The session expiry notification became one of the most important UX additions — not just for business reasons (AI token costs) but because sessions ending silently was one of the most frustrating moments in testing. The countdown modal with "Sí, continuar / Salir de la sesión" gave users control and removed a major source of confusion.

We also added a post-session feedback form — essential for an MVP that needed to keep learning. Users rate clarity of responses and usefulness of the interaction, and can leave a comment. The data feeds directly into ongoing avatar improvement.

09 · Learnings

What this project taught me

1

Designing for low AI familiarity means designing for trust first, features second. Before users care about what the avatar can do, they need to feel safe enough to try.

2

Familiar interaction patterns lower the barrier to adoption. Framing the experience as a video call — something everyone has done — meant users understood how to use it without any instruction.

3

Cultural specificity is a design decision. Making Mia visibly Mexican, Spanish-speaking with a Mexican accent, and professional but warm wasn't superficial — it was a core part of making the product feel made for its users.

4

System state communication is everything in voice UI. Unlike a visual form, the user has no visual queue for what's happening — every microphone state, loading moment, and session event needs to be communicated clearly and immediately.

5

Testing across a real age range reveals real problems. Our oldest participant, Gabriela (71), surfaced issues that younger users didn't encounter — and those were the issues that mattered most for the target audience.
