01 · Context

The problem: a tedious,
paper-like experience in a digital world

Lockton's policy holders had to fill out complex medical expense reimbursement forms manually — a process that was long, repetitive, and full of technical insurance language that most users didn't understand. The client came to us with a clear goal: create a new digital experience that made this process easier and faster.

Our proposed solution was an OCR/AI-powered system embedded directly into Lockton's existing medical expenses platform. Instead of typing everything by hand, users would upload their documents and the system would extract and pre-fill the form data automatically.
02 · Research

What users actually struggled with

Before touching any design tool, I ran a research phase combining competitive benchmarking and surveys targeted at people who regularly deal with medical claims and insurance forms. The findings were consistent across respondents:

Finding 01
Time-consuming repetition

Users had to re-enter information already present in their receipts and prescriptions — every single time.

Finding 02
Confusing terminology

Medical and insurance jargon made users unsure what was being asked, leading to errors and re-submissions.

Finding 03
No sense of progress

Users couldn't tell how far along they were, or what steps remained — creating anxiety and drop-off.

Finding 04
Lack of guidance

When something went wrong, there was no contextual help. Users had to call support or start over.

"It feels like filling out a tax form, but nobody explains what anything means."

— Survey respondent, frequent insurance claims user
Benchmark conclusion: The benchmark confirmed that competitors had a similar opportunity gap: most platforms lacked meaningful guidance, clear language, and any form of automation. This validated that even incremental improvements in these areas could represent a significant differentiator.
03 · User Personas

Two users with very different needs

From the research, two distinct user archetypes emerged — each with different comfort levels with technology, different priorities, and different failure points.

Power User
Laura, la Ejecutiva Ocupada
38 · Ops Manager · High digital literacy
Goals & pain points
  • Wants to finish in under 15 minutes
  • Trusts automation to be accurate
  • Frustrated by re-entering data she already has on paper
  • Needs a "fast track" for pre-validated submissions
Cautious User
Roberto, el Cuidador Familiar
49 · SME Owner · Intermediate digital literacy
Goals & pain points
  • Needs clear step-by-step guidance
  • Anxious about submitting wrong documents
  • Frustrated by vague error messages
  • Needs to understand exactly what each field means
04 · Design Process

Mapping the flow, then designing around it

In collaboration with Lockton, we mapped the complete user flow, balancing the insurer's data requirements with our goal of making the process as quick as possible. We identified a 5-step structure:

1
Configuration

User selects the type of claim and affected insured. Pre-filled data used when available from prior claims.

2
Information

Medical details form — reason for visit, diagnosis, symptoms, hospital, and doctor. AI tooltips guide each field.

3
Documents

User uploads ID, proof of address, bank statement, and invoices. OCR reads and extracts data automatically.

4
Validation

User reviews pre-filled personal data and can correct any OCR errors before final submission.

5
Download

Filled form is generated and available for download. The assistant explains next steps clearly.

05 · Key Design Decisions

The choices that shaped the experience

The AI assistant as a visible, friendly presence

Rather than hiding the AI in the background, I made it a small animated character — a glowing blue blob — visible throughout the entire process. This served a dual purpose: it made users feel accompanied and guided, and it made the AI system feel approachable rather than opaque. The character was deliberately soft, simple, and non-threatening.

Contextual tooltips above the assistant's head

Every form field that could cause confusion triggers a tooltip that appears above the AI character — not as a pop-up interrupting the flow, but as a natural "bubble" from the assistant. This preserved the guidance metaphor while keeping the interface clean.

A loading animation that communicates, not just waits

During the OCR processing phase, instead of a generic spinner, the assistant visually "reads" a document. This small detail reframes waiting time as active work being done on the user's behalf, building trust and encouraging patience.

Pre-filled data with an editable validation step

After the OCR runs, users land on a verification screen where they can review and correct any data the AI extracted. This acknowledges that AI isn't perfect and empowers the user — especially cautious users like Roberto — to feel in control without slowing down confident users like Laura.

White-label aesthetic within Lockton's design system

I explored moodboards and settled on a clean, modern medical platform aesthetic — light backgrounds, blue gradient accents, generous whitespace. Crucially, I achieved this new visual feel without breaking Lockton's existing UI design system, ensuring consistency across their platform and smooth developer handoff.

Real-time notifications and email delivery

The filled form is available for download immediately after the process, and also delivered via email. This accommodates both impatient users who want it now and those who prefer to retrieve it later — removing the pressure to act instantly.

08 · Learnings

What this project taught me

  • 1

    Making AI visible — not invisible — builds trust. Users are more patient and forgiving when they understand something is working on their behalf.

  • 2

    The correction step is not a failure mode; it's a feature. Designing for AI imperfection upfront creates a better experience than pretending the AI is always right.

  • 3

    Working within an existing design system forces creative constraints that often produce better, more cohesive results than designing from scratch.

  • 4

    Two very different personas can share one flow — as long as the design accommodates both speeds: fast-track for the confident user, guided detail for the cautious one.

Daphne Hidalgo · UX/UI Designer
